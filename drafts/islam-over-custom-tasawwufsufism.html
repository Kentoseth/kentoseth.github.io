Islam over custom: Tasawwuf/Sufism

Posted on Fri 02 December 2022 in Islam

بِسْمِ ٱللَّٰهِ ٱلرَّحْمَٰنِ ٱلرَّحِيمِ

The primary goal of this series is not to discuss jurisprudence, but to highlight how certain Islamic practices have evolved or their status elevated and the resulting effects on the South African Muslim community.

The primary audience for this series is fellow Muslims. Where possible, I will do my best to educate non-Muslims about certain terms.

Background

In my previous article, i discussed various issues about Taraweeh. i intended to make the next article about Ramadan, but the more pressing need for me was to discuss this topic of Tasawwuf.

The words Sufism and Tasawwuf are usually used interchangeably, but as the former is used in derogatory terms, i will only use the latter in the article below.

What does Tasawwuf actually mean?

For the vast majority of people from Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah (and some Shias) it is defined as an effort to reform one-self (the inner self) spiritually.

For the group who self-identify themselves as 'Salafi', Tasawwuf as a whole is considered an innovation (and maybe even heresy). this article is not intended to discuss the concerns of modernist movements, but to instead discuss glaring issues that seem to be ignored, overlooked or (I fear) willfully accepted without introspection nor investigation.

History

The history of Tasawwuf unquestionably can be traced all the way back to the Prophet Mohammed(PBUH). Nobody can deny that luminaries like Hasan Al-Basri(ra), who was a Tabi'ee, were linked to the Sufi tradition.

While researching this topic, i asked myself two questions:

  • is Tasawwuf a book based knowledge or is it primarily experiential?
  • is there a structured path or means to follow?

It seems that there does exist a book based component wherein certain works of Imam Al-Ghazali(ra) are studied for beginners. but this is not universal across Sufi practice.

At the same time it could be argued that the study of specific books (within the realm of Tasawwuf) is not required, as a thorough study of the various Islamic Sciences would expose one to the aspects of inner reformation.

But as the vast majority of followers of a Tariqa (spiritual order) are not students of the islamic sciences nor is a book based approach universal, this leaves a significant gap that could either allow for flexibility or for one to go astray.

Jurisprudence

My exposure to modern Arabs made me aware of the fact that many of them deem Tasawwuf (via the name Sufi) to be its own sect, in the vain of: Sunni, Sufi, Salafi, Shia

This is a mind-boggling insinuation as it creates the impression that the practioners of self-reformation are following other than the majority (Ahlus Sunnah) in spite af the fact that the vast majority of these practioners are from the 4 jurisprudential schools and followers of the 3 main schools of creed.
